# Workforce planning and hiring

Two connected jobs: decide whether and whom to hire (Workforce Planning), then set the role and process up to hire well (Hiring Prep). The bar throughout: earn every hire, then run a process that scores signal.

## Workforce Planning

### 1. Gap analysis
Compare the roles the target Organization Model implies to who exists today. For each area: is it adequately staffed, overloaded, or empty? Use the team-ops frictions (bottlenecks, overloaded teams) as corroborating evidence.

### 2. Need detection (honest)
A real need is a scoped gap that blocks the strategy, tied to specific work it unblocks. "We are busy" is not a need; "no one owns billing and three deals are stalled on it" is. State the work each gap unblocks and the cost of leaving it open.

### 3. Options per gap (build vs hire vs ...)
Never jump straight to "hire". For each gap weigh:

- **Build:** develop or promote someone internal. Cheapest culturally, slower, needs slack.
- **Hire:** add a person. Specify level (junior/senior/lead) and type (specialist vs generalist), and why this level fits the gap and stage.
- **Reassign:** move scope between existing people/teams.
- **Outsource / contract:** for non-core or spiky work.
- **Defer:** if the gap does not yet block the strategy.

Recommend per gap with trade-offs, rough cost, and time-to-impact.

### 4. Sequencing
Order hires by dependency and stage: a manager before their reports, a platform/enabling capability before scaling teams that depend on it, revenue-unblocking roles before nice-to-haves. Check affordability against runway and the financials where known (via the data room or Founder-OS); flag, do not invent, numbers.

### 5. Deliver
A prioritized workforce plan: each gap, recommended option, reasoning, sequence position, and cost/runway implication. Confirmed hires route to Hiring Prep.

## Hiring Prep

### Role definition
From the gap: mission (why the role exists), scope, the outcomes it must deliver in the first 6 to 12 months, level, and how it fits the Organization Model (which team, which interfaces).

### Job description
Lead with mission and outcomes, not a task list. Separate must-have from nice-to-have (keep must-haves few and real). State level, compensation band (mark where the user must set the numbers; functional-hr-ops frames, it does not set pay), location, and work mode. Write it to attract the right person and deter the wrong one, honestly.

### Scorecard
The few outcomes and competencies that actually predict success in this role, each with "what good looks like". This is what interviewers score against, so signal beats vibes. Keep it short and role-specific.

### Interview plan
The loop: stages (for example screen, craft/skills, team/values, founder), who assesses what, mapped to the scorecard so each competency is covered once well. Provide the key questions or exercise per stage. Avoid redundant stages and unstructured chats.

### Candidate prospectus
The brief sent to candidates to sell the opportunity with candor: company and mission, the role's real impact, the team and who they will work with, stage and trajectory, compensation framing, and what success looks like in the first year. Honest about challenges, not hype. This is the document the user asked for to present the opportunity to candidates.

### Deliver
Role definition, JD, scorecard, interview plan, and candidate prospectus, ready to use. Render to docs/PDF where useful. Employment-law specifics (contracts, regulations) route to a legal skill or counsel; functional-hr-ops provides structure and drafts, not legal advice.
